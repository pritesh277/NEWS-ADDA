The southwest monsoon arrived in Kerala on 24 May — the earliest since 2009 — and swept across the entire country by 29 June, nine days ahead of schedule. While the rapid advance initially brought relief from summer heat and allowed farmers to sow crops early, it also caught many regions unprepared for the sheer intensity of rain that followed. Nationally, May rainfall was more than double the long-term average, with central India recording its wettest May ever and the south peninsula its second wettest. Himachal Pradesh endured one of its worst monsoons in decades, with relentless rain triggering massive landslides, washing away homes, and cutting off entire mountain communities from electricity, drinking water, and medical access. Rajasthan, typically arid, was transformed into a flood zone, with mud-built homes collapsing, farmland submerged, and livestock lost. Punjab’s fertile fields were inundated, submerging over 200 villages and displacing thousands, while Uttar Pradesh, Jammu & Kashmir, and Haryana faced swollen rivers, cloudbursts, and infrastructure damage. Experts link the severity to climate change–driven shifts in rainfall patterns, with multiple western disturbances — extra-tropical storms intensified by warming in West Asia and the Arctic — colliding with the monsoon to unleash concentrated bursts of rain. These events have not only claimed more than 700 lives nationwide but also inflicted long-term damage on agriculture, infrastructure, and livelihoods, underscoring the urgent need for climate resilience and disaster preparedness in the years ahead.





Supreme Court at Full Strength

In a landmark development on August 29, 2025, the Supreme Court of India swore in Justices Alok Aradhe and Vipul M. Pancholi, restoring the apex court to its full sanctioned strength of 34 judges under the Collegium system established by Articles 124 and 217 of the Constitution. This milestone—up from the original eight‐judge bench at its inception in 1950—enables the Court to constitution more benches concurrently, significantly reduce a backlog that often exceeds 80,000 pending cases, and empanel larger Constitution benches for issues of momentous constitutional import, thereby streamlining judicial processes and reinforcing the mandate of timely justice. The Collegium mechanism itself emerged from the First Judges Case of 1981 and subsequent rulings, vesting appointment recommendations in a panel led by the Chief Justice of India alongside four senior colleagues, and striking a balance between judicial independence and accountability. Achieving full strength not only lightens the workload on sitting judges but also bolsters public confidence in the judiciary’s capacity to uphold fundamental rights and deliver equitable justice across civil, criminal, and constitutional domains.
